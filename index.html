🤔 How portal linking actually works

Portals don't remember each other. Every single time you step through, the game re-runs the same little ritual — five simple rules. Once you know them, you can never be surprised again.

1

The Nether is 8× smaller

Walking 1 block in the Nether = 8 blocks in the Overworld. That's why nether highways are so fast.

When you enter a portal, the game divides your X and Z by 8 (or multiplies by 8 going the other way) to find where you should come out. Height (Y) is never scaled.

2

The game searches a square zone

Around that ÷8 spot, the game looks for existing portals in a square area: ±16 blocks sideways in the Nether (±128 in the Overworld, on Java 1.16+).

Height doesn't matter for being found — a portal way up on the nether roof is still spotted if its X/Z are inside the square.

3

Closest portal wins — in 3D

If several portals are inside the square, the game picks the one closest to your arrival spot in full 3D — and here, height suddenly does count.

A portal on the nether roof (Y≈128) starts with a ~40-block distance handicap against ground-level portals. Smart players use this on purpose to control links!

4

Your exact standing spot matters (a little)

A portal is 2 blocks wide, and the game uses your position — not the portal's — for the ÷8 math. Stand on the other side of the frame and the arrival spot can shift by 1 block.

Usually harmless… unless your plan only wins by a hair. Good plans keep a 2–3 block safety margin so they work no matter where you stand.

5

Link stealing — and how to beat it

Two bases close together in the Overworld? Their ÷8 arrival spots in the Nether end up right next to each other — and one nether portal can sit in both search squares, hijacking its neighbour's traffic.

The fix: nudge one portal so it's outside the other base's square (more than 16 blocks from its arrival spot) while staying inside its own. Then each base can only ever find its own portal. That's exactly what our Advanced planner figures out for you.
